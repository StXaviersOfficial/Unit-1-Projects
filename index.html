Vocational Education What is Vocational Education? Vocational Education is a type of education that provides students with practical skills and hands-on training to prepare them for specific jobs and careers. Unlike traditional education that focuses only on theory, vocational education focuses on learning by doing. It covers areas such as agriculture, health care, technology, handicrafts, and home science. Importance of Vocational Education
  • Develops practical life skills useful in everyday situations.
  • Prepares students for employment immediately after school.
  • Promotes scientific thinking and problem-solving abilities.
  • Encourages environmental awareness and sustainable practices.
  • Helps students understand the connection between science and real life.
  • Builds confidence and creativity through project-based learning.
  • Supports India's Skill India Mission — training youth for a stronger economy.

Project 1 : Hydroponics — Introduction Introduction Hydroponics is a modern, scientifically advanced method of cultivating plants without using traditional soil. Instead, plants are grown in water enriched with essential nutrients, supplying all the minerals and conditions required for vigorous, healthy growth in a clean, controlled environment. In this project, students explore how plants can thrive using only water, light, and carefully prepared nutrient solutions. They investigate different hydroponic systems, observe plant development over time, and appreciate how technology can revolutionise agriculture — especially in areas with limited fertile soil or scarce water resources. Learning Outcomes By the end of this project, I learnt to :
  • Grow healthy microgreens using a basic hydroponics setup.
  • Establish Wick, Deep Water Culture (DWC), and NFT systems.
  • Prepare organic liquid manure (compost tea).
  • Improve water quality by accurately adjusting pH level.
  • Appreciate sustainable and eco-friendly farming methods.

Activity 1 — Growing Microgreens for a Healthy Salad Introduction
Growing microgreens is one of the easiest and most rewarding ways for students to understand how nutritious food can be produced within just a few days. Microgreens are young, tender plants harvested shortly after germination and are densely packed with vitamins and minerals. This activity allows students to experience the complete cycle — from seed selection and tray preparation to caring for sprouts and finally harvesting fresh microgreens to prepare a wholesome, healthy salad.
Procedure
  • Take a plastic tray (10×30 cm) and make small drainage holes at the bottom.
  • Fill 2 inches of cocopeat or tissue paper and moisten evenly using a spray bottle.
  • Sprinkle seeds (spinach, fenugreek) uniformly — do not overcrowd.
  • Cover lightly with soil or tissue; place in shade for 1–2 days for germination.
  • Mist gently once or twice daily to keep the surface moist, never soggy.
  • After germination, move tray near sunlight or under an LED grow light.
  • Harvest after 7–10 days when plants reach 3.5 cm — use clean scissors.

Activity 2 — Building a Hydroponic System Using the Wick Method Introduction
The Wick Method is the simplest and most beginner-friendly way to grow plants without soil. In this activity, students learn how to build a basic hydroponic system using easily available materials — a container, wicks, and a growing medium. By assembling this system, students understand how plants absorb water and nutrients through capillary action and grow efficiently without soil. This activity introduces modern, sustainable farming techniques and fosters curiosity, creativity, and scientific thinking.
Procedure
  • Take a used 2L PET bottle; cut it into two equal halves using scissors.
  • Make a small hole in the bottle cap and insert a cotton wick through it.
  • Invert the top half — it becomes the plant holder. Fill it with cocopeat.
  • The bottom half is the reservoir. Fill it with prepared nutrient solution.
  • Place the inverted top into the reservoir so the wick dips into the solution.
  • Sow seeds or seedlings in the cocopeat, placing them around the wick.
  • Keep the setup near sunlight and refill the nutrient solution as it depletes.

Activity 3 — Building a Hydroponic System Using Deep Water Culture (DWC) Introduction
In the Deep Water Culture (DWC) system, plant roots are suspended directly in nutrient-rich water, and oxygen is continuously supplied through an aerator to ensure healthy root development. This method is simple, cost-effective, and ideal for growing leafy vegetables such as spinach. By using recycled materials like buckets, plastic sheets, and thermocol, students create a fully functional hydroponic garden at home or school — experiencing the science of plant nutrition firsthand.
Procedure
  • Take a 15-litre bucket; ensure it does not leak and clean it thoroughly.
  • Create a lid using a plastic sheet or thermocol and cut round holes in it.
  • Place net cups (filled with cocopeat/sand) firmly into each hole.
  • Fill the bucket with nutrient-rich water so it just touches the base of the cups.
  • Place an aerator (fish tank pump) inside the bucket to dissolve oxygen.
  • Plant 2–3 spinach seedlings in each cup; maintain 5–10 cm spacing.
  • Monitor water level, aerator function, and root colour daily. White roots = healthy.

Activity 4 — Building a Hydroponic System Using Nutrient Film Technique (NFT) Introduction
The Nutrient Film Technique (NFT) is one of the most efficient and commercially used hydroponic systems worldwide. A thin, continuous film of nutrient-rich water flows through inclined channels or PVC pipes, bathing the roots of plants as it passes. Students assemble this system to understand how plants absorb nutrients through flowing water, how to design a water-efficient growing setup, and how modern farming techniques can maximise food production even in compact urban spaces.
Procedure
  • Use 1-metre PVC pipes (2–3 inch diameter); drill evenly spaced holes along the top.
  • Arrange channels on a metal or wooden frame at a 2–4% slope for continuous flow.
  • Set up a nutrient reservoir (20–40 litres) filled with hydroponic nutrient solution.
  • Place a submersible pump in the reservoir; connect a delivery tube to the top channel.
  • Fit a return pipe at the channel outlet to recirculate solution back to the reservoir.
  • Insert net pots filled with clay pebbles and seedlings into the drilled holes.
  • Switch on the pump — verify a thin film of water flows along each channel floor.
  • Maintain pH between 5.5–6.5 and replace nutrient solution every 1–2 weeks.

Activity 5 — Making Compost Tea (Organic Liquid Fertilizer) Introduction
Compost tea is a nutrient-rich liquid fertilizer produced by soaking mature compost in water, extracting beneficial microorganisms and plant nutrients in the process. It acts as a powerful natural fertilizer and plant booster that helps plants grow healthier and stronger without relying on harmful chemical fertilizers. Through this activity, students learn how organic waste can be transformed into a sustainable, eco-friendly resource that improves soil health and enriches the soil ecosystem.
Procedure
  • Take well-finished, mature compost — dark, crumbly, with a pleasant earthy smell.
  • Fill a clean bucket with 10 litres of non-chlorinated or dechlorinated water.
  • Put 150 g of compost into a muslin cloth bag; tie it tightly with string.
  • Place the compost bag into the bucket of water.
  • Add 50 g of jaggery or molasses to encourage microbial growth; stir thoroughly.
  • Place an aquarium air pump to aerate the mixture continuously for 24–36 hours.
  • Let the compost steep for 24–48 hours — the water will turn rich brown.
  • Remove the compost bag, squeeze gently to collect remaining liquid; tea is ready.

Activity 6 — Maintaining Water pH for Optimal Plant Growth Introduction
Maintaining the correct pH level of water is absolutely essential for healthy plant growth — especially in hydroponic systems where water is the sole medium for supplying nutrients. pH is a measure of how acidic or alkaline a solution is. Nutrient mobility is best when water pH is in the range of 6.5 to 7.0. Even a small deviation can drastically affect how well plants absorb essential nutrients. In this activity, learners gain practical experience in measuring, analysing, and adjusting pH using precise scientific tools.
Procedure
  • Collect a water sample from the hydroponic system in a clean container.
  • Calibrate the digital pH meter using standard buffer solutions (pH 4, 7, 10).
  • Dip the pH meter probe into the water; wait for the reading to stabilise.
  • If pH is too low (acidic) → add pH Up solution drop by drop and stir.
  • If pH is too high (alkaline) → add vinegar (pH Down) drop by drop and stir.
  • Re-test after each addition; never add large amounts at once.
  • Once pH reaches 6.5–7.0, the water is ready for the hydroponic system.
  • Rinse the pH meter probe with distilled water after every use; store carefully.
