Solar Energy — Project What is Solar Energy? Solar energy is the energy obtained from the sun's radiation. The sun is a giant nuclear reactor releasing enormous amounts of energy in the form of light and heat. This energy can be captured and converted into electricity or used for heating purposes using solar panels and solar cells. Solar energy is renewable and clean — it produces no harmful gases and will never run out as long as the sun shines. How Does Solar Energy Work? Solar panels are made of photovoltaic (PV) cells. When sunlight falls on these cells, it knocks electrons loose and creates an electric current — this is called the Photovoltaic Effect. The electricity produced can power homes, schools, and industries. India and Solar Energy India receives about 300 sunny days per year, making it one of the best countries for solar energy. India ranks among the top 5 nations globally in installed solar capacity.
5
Applications of Solar Energy
  • Solar Panels — convert sunlight into electricity for homes and industries.
  • Solar Water Heaters — heat water without using electricity or gas.
  • Solar Street Lights — light up roads using energy stored in the day.
  • Solar Cookers — use sunlight to cook food, saving fuel and money.
  • Solar Pumps — help farmers irrigate fields without electricity bills.
  • Solar Vehicles — cars and boats running on solar energy reduce pollution.
India's National Solar Mission (NSM) The National Solar Mission was launched in 2010 under India's National Action Plan on Climate Change to make India a global leader in solar energy.
  • Target of 500 GW solar capacity by 2030.
  • Reduce dependence on coal and fossil fuels.
  • Promote rooftop solar panels on homes and institutions.
  • Create jobs in the solar energy sector across India.
6
Advantages of Solar Energy
  • It is renewable — the sun will shine for billions of years.
  • It produces no pollution — no smoke, no greenhouse gases.
  • It significantly reduces electricity bills for households.
  • It can power remote areas where grid electricity does not reach.
  • It requires very low maintenance once the panels are installed.
Disadvantages of Solar Energy
  • Cannot generate power at night or during heavily cloudy days.
  • Initial installation cost of solar panels is relatively high.
  • Solar farms require large areas of land.
Conclusion Solar energy is the energy of the future. As fossil fuels deplete and climate change threatens our planet, India must shift to clean and renewable sources like solar energy. With its abundant sunshine, India has the potential to become a world leader in solar power and build a greener, healthier planet for all.

Project 1 : Hydroponics — Introduction Introduction Hydroponics is a modern method of growing plants without using soil. Instead of soil, plants are grown in water enriched with essential nutrients. This technique provides all the minerals and conditions required for healthy plant growth in a clean and controlled environment. In this project, students explore how plants can grow using only water, light, and nutrient solutions. They learn about different hydroponic systems and observe plant growth over time. Learning Outcomes By the end of this project, I learnt to :
  • Grow healthy microgreens using a basic hydroponics setup.
  • Establish Wick, Deep Water Culture (DWC), and NFT systems.
  • Prepare organic liquid manure (compost tea).
  • Improve water quality by adjusting pH level.
  • Understand sustainable and eco-friendly farming methods.

Activity 1 — Growing Microgreens Procedure
  • Take a plastic tray (10×30 cm) and make small drainage holes at the bottom.
  • Fill 2 inches of cocopeat or tissue paper and moisten with water using a spray bottle.
  • Spread seeds uniformly over the moist surface — do not overcrowd them.
  • Cover lightly with soil or tissue. Keep in a shaded area for 1–2 days for germination.
  • Mist once or twice daily to keep the surface moist but not soggy.
  • After germination, move to sunlight or under an LED grow light.
  • Harvest after 7–10 days when plants are about 3.5 cm tall using clean scissors.

Activity 2 — Wick Method Procedure
  • Take a used 2L PET bottle and cut it into two equal halves.
  • Make a small hole in the bottle cap and insert a cotton wick through it.
  • Invert the top half — it becomes the plant holder. Fill it with cocopeat.
  • The bottom half is the reservoir. Fill it with nutrient solution.
  • Place the inverted top half into the reservoir so the wick dips into the solution.
  • Plant seeds or seedlings in the cocopeat, around the wick.
  • Keep near sunlight. Refill the nutrient solution when level drops.

Activity 3 — Deep Water Culture (DWC) Procedure
  • Take a 15-litre bucket and make round holes in the lid using a hole saw cutter.
  • Place net cups (filled with cocopeat/sand) into each hole firmly.
  • Fill the bucket with nutrient-rich water so it just touches the base of the net cups.
  • Place an aerator (fish tank aerator) inside the bucket to dissolve oxygen in the water.
  • Plant 2–3 spinach seedlings in each net cup; maintain 5–10 cm spacing.
  • Check water level, aerator function, and root colour regularly.
  • Healthy roots should be white. Brown roots indicate lack of dissolved oxygen.

Activity 4
Nutrient Film Technique
(NFT) System
NFT — thin film of nutrient solution flows continuously through PVC channels
Duration : 30 Days  |  Crop : Lettuce (8 plants)
Growth : Fast  |  Leaf Colour : Dark Green
18
Activity 4 — Nutrient Film Technique (NFT) Procedure
  • Use 1-metre PVC pipes (2–3 inches diameter) as growing channels. Drill evenly spaced holes.
  • Arrange channels on a frame at a slight slope (2–4%) so water flows continuously.
  • Set up a nutrient reservoir (20–40 litres) with hydroponic nutrient solution.
  • Place a submersible pump inside the reservoir and connect it to the top channel inlet.
  • Put a return pipe at the channel outlet to bring solution back to the reservoir.
  • Place net pots filled with clay pebbles and seedlings into the channel holes.
  • Switch on the pump — check that a thin film of water flows along each channel floor.
  • Maintain pH between 5.5 and 6.5. Replace nutrient solution every 1–2 weeks.

Activity 5 — Making Compost Tea Procedure
  • Take well-finished, mature compost — dark, crumbly, with an earthy smell.
  • Fill a clean bucket with 10 litres of non-chlorinated (or dechlorinated) water.
  • Put 150 g of compost into a muslin cloth bag and tie it tightly.
  • Place the compost bag into the bucket of water.
  • Add 50 g of jaggery or molasses to encourage microbial growth; stir well.
  • Place an aquarium air pump to aerate the mixture for 24–36 hours.
  • Stir the mixture 2–3 times a day if no pump is used.
  • Let the compost steep for 24–48 hours — the water turns brown.
  • Remove the compost bag, squeeze gently; the compost tea is ready to use.

Activity 6 — Maintaining Water pH Why pH Matters in Hydroponics pH is a measure of how acidic or alkaline water is. The ideal pH range for most hydroponic plants is 5.5–6.5. If pH is too low (acidic), water harms roots. If too high (alkaline), plants cannot absorb nutrients properly and growth is stunted. Procedure
  • Collect a water sample from the hydroponic system in a clean bucket.
  • Dip a digital pH meter probe into the water and note the reading.
  • If pH is too low → add pH Up solution (alkaline) drop by drop.
  • If pH is too high → add pH Down solution (e.g., vinegar) drop by drop.
  • Stir the water and re-test after each addition. Never add too much at once.
  • Once pH reaches 6.5–7.0, the water is ready for the hydroponic system.
  • Rinse the pH meter probe with distilled water after use and store safely.
